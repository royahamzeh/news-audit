'I thought I was going to die' - Venezuelans describe earthquake panic

In Venezuela, residents of the capital were gripped by fear and panic as two powerful earthquakes struck the area within seconds of each other. The first earthquake registered a magnitude of 7.2, followed closely by an even more powerful quake at 7.5. Witnesses describe chaotic scenes as people fled buildings, fearing imminent collapse. The city's infrastructure, though accustomed to seismic activity, was unable to fully withstand the force of the dual tremors. Emergency services responded swiftly, but the sheer scale of the quakes posed significant challenges. Many Venezuelans recounted vivid experiences, with one survivor stating, 'I thought I was going to die.' The psychological impact of the quakes is profound, particularly in a nation already beset by economic and political turmoil. Despite the destruction, there were stories of community solidarity, as neighbors and strangers alike reached out to support one another amid the chaos. Authorities are continuing rescue and relief operations, but the path to recovery is expected to be long and arduous. Experts warn of potential aftershocks, urging residents to remain vigilant. As Venezuela grapples with the aftermath, there is an urgent call for international assistance to help rebuild and provide psychological support to the affected population.
