Bowen: Iran deal ends Trump's war that revealed limit of US dominance

The recent agreement to conclude hostilities between the United States and Iran marks a significant yet sobering moment in international relations. Brokered to re-open the Strait of Hormuz, the accord essentially resets the geopolitical stage to the conditions that existed just a day prior to the onset of conflict. While the immediate resumption of normalcy in one of the world's most crucial maritime pathways brings relief to global markets, the haunting reminder of war's devastating toll lingers heavily. Experts suggest the casualties inflicted over the brief but intense period of engagement far outweigh any conceivable diplomatic gains achieved by the brief show of force. This episode underscores a profound reality: the era of uncontested American dominance might be fading, as military confrontations no longer guarantee strategic supremacy. Trump's hardline stance, intended to reaffirm US authority, seemingly revealed the boundaries of such doctrine, with thousands now dead and no notable shifts in the balance of power. Politicians and military analysts alike are calling for comprehensive evaluations, hoping to draw lessons from the conflict that might inform future policy. As the dust settles, the world watches closely to see if this fragile peace can foster a new era of dialogue and stability in the region.
