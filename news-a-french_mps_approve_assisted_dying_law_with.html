French MPs approve assisted dying law with strict rules after years of argument

French MPs have passed a landmark bill allowing assisted dying for terminally ill adults under stringent conditions following years of heated debate. The newly approved legislation represents a significant shift in France's approach to end-of-life care, aligning it more closely with the practices already established in countries like Belgium and the Netherlands. Under the new law, individuals wishing to end their lives must adhere to strict criteria: they must be adults, have a terminal illness with no prospect of recovery, and be in a state of constant, unbearable suffering. Additionally, the decision to proceed with assisted dying will require repeated confirmation of the patient's consent and comprehensive consultations with medical professionals to ensure that all alternatives have been considered. Though the law has been praised by advocates of personal choice and dignity in dying, it has also sparked controversy, particularly among religious groups and others who fear it might lead to ethical and societal repercussions. As France moves forward with implementing this law, debates continue regarding its moral implications, the adequacy of palliative care, and the necessity of maintaining rigorous safeguards to prevent potential abuses or unintended consequences.
